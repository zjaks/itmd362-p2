The 15 Greatest Moments In Chicago Sports History

15. Steve Bartman- public enemy number one

October 14, 2003

In one of the most infamous moments in sports history, the Cubs were finally within earshot of making it to the World Series in 2003. Five outs is all that stood between the Cubs and their goal. In a crazy turn of events during the top of the 8th inning, a foul ball hit down the left field line suddenly was taken away from Moises Alou's glove. Alou, visibly upset, added fuel to the fire of the angry fans. A man named Steve Bartman was the one who went for that ball, and the rest is history. Things only unraveled from there. A number of timely hits by the Marlins and costly errors from the Cubs quickly unraveled a 3-0 series-clinching victory into a 8-3 stunning defeat. As things continued to unravel, fans began to yell and throw things at Bartman, who quickly needed to be escorted from Wrigley Field. In one of Chicago sport's darkest moments, fans were out for blood for the young Bartman. The next game the Cubs lost as well, losing the NLCS and bringing a World-Series bound team to elimination. Bartman, taking 100% of the blame, quickly became the most hated man in Chicago.

14. Patrick Kane brings Blackhawks first Stanley Cup in 49 years

June 9, 2010

The Blackhawks won their first Stanley Cup in 49 years in a very confusing fashion. The club was caught in a tense overtime against the Philadelphia Flyers, and Patrick Kane fired a shot from a seemingly impossible angle. The puck got caught in the top corner of the net, leaving onlookers confused as to what had happened. Kane immediately threw his gloves off and began dancing across the ice. After a brief video review, officials verified that the Blackhawks were Stanley Cup champions, sending relief to many lifelong fans waiting for this moment to happen again, and it was just a storybook beginning for the Blackhawks.

13. Sammy Sosa hits home runs 61 and 62, passes Maris

September 13, 1998

The 1990's and early 2000's was considered one of the best eras of baseball. in 1998, Sammy Sosa and rival from the St. Louis Cardinals Mark McGwire found themselves in a home run race for the ages. In what seemed like a back and forth display of power night in and night out, both players were looking to break the single-season home run record set by Roger Maris in 1961 with 61 home runs in a single season. With McGwire passing Maris' record just 5 days earlier, it was then Sosa's turn to shine. In the same game Sosa belted home runs number 61 AND 62, tying and passing Maris as well. This was the capstone of an amazing season for baseball, only to be unearthed and shunned several years later due to many players caught using steroids and performance enhancing substances. Sosa finished 1998 with 66 home runs, where McGwire set the record at 70, only to fall a few years later to another controversial PED user, Barry Bonds, whose record still stands at 73 home runs in a single season.

12. Walter Payton breaks the all-time rushing record

October 7, 1984

Considered by many as the greatest running back, and by some the greatest football player of all time, Chicago Bears running back Walter Payton became the NFL’s all-time rushing leader in 1984, breaking the record Cleveland’s Jim Brown set in 1965. In front of 53,752 people at Soldier Field, Payton carried the ball for 154 yards and finished the game well beyond Brown's old record. During the second quarter of the game, Payton made nine runs and gained 30 yards, including a one-yard touchdown with three seconds left in the half. To beat Brown’s record, he needed to carry the ball just three more yards. Almost as soon as the second half began, Bears quarterback Jim McMahon pitched the ball to Payton in a reliable play that the team called the “Toss 28 Weak.” Fullback Matt Suhey and left guard Mark Bortz protected Payton as he completed a record-setting six-yard run. He didn’t take much time to celebrate, though. All week, Bears officials had been urging their star to let them stop the game for a ceremony when he finally broke the rushing record, but Payton maintained that the team’s momentum was more important than his achievement. So, the festivities were brief. Payton accepted his teammates’ congratulatory hugs and handshakes, turned the record-setting football over to a representative from the Pro Football Hall of Fame, and went back to the game. (He did take a call from President Ronald Reagan afterwards.) His dedication paid off: The Bears won the game 20-7 and Payton set one more record, for carrying the ball more than 100 yards for the 59th time in his career.

11. "The Shot"

May 7, 1989

In 1989, Michael Jordan had yet to win an NBA title and was still trying to prove himself as one of the league's best players. In the Bulls' 1989 first round playoff series against the Cleveland Cavaliers, he quieted some of his critics. The Bulls were down by one point with three seconds remaining in the game. Jordan took the inbound pass and hit a buzzer-beating shot to bring the team into the second round to the playoffs. The Bulls did not win a title that season, but "The Shot" became remembered as one of Jordan's most clutch moments in his career. It was replayed on television hundreds of times, to the agony of Cleveland fans and the ecstasy of Bulls supporters. It was only one basket, yet it played an integral role in the fortunes of not one but two NBA franchises.

10. Kerry Wood strikes out 20

May 6, 1998

Few pitchers had the phenom hype like Kerry Wood had in 1998. In only his fifth major league start, he did something that still has yet to be beaten until this day. Facing the Houston Astros, who at the time were one of the best hitting teams in the league, Wood had a dominating day. The Astros managed one infield single and Wood hit a batter, so it wasn't a perfect game or even a no-hitter, if it was, it might have been the greatest game ever pitched. This sparked the career of Kerry Wood and a short time where the Cubs had real hope at a title run. You can see and hear the electricity in the air that day in the video above. This was an exciting time in Cubs history, and one that will always be remembered.
