The 15 Greatest Moments In Chicago Sports History

15. Steve Bartman- public enemy number one

October 14, 2003

In one of the most infamous moments in sports history, the Cubs were finally within earshot of making it to the World Series in 2003. Five outs is all that stood between the Cubs and their goal. In a crazy turn of events during the top of the 8th inning, a foul ball hit down the left field line suddenly was taken away from Moises Alou's glove. Alou, visibly upset, added fuel to the fire of the angry fans. A man named Steve Bartman was the one who went for that ball, and the rest is history. Things only unraveled from there. A number of timely hits by the Marlins and costly errors from the Cubs quickly unraveled a 3-0 series-clinching victory into a 8-3 stunning defeat. As things continued to unravel, fans began to yell and throw things at Bartman, who quickly needed to be escorted from Wrigley Field. In one of Chicago sport's darkest moments, fans were out for blood for the young Bartman. The next game the Cubs lost as well, losing the NLCS and bringing a World-Series bound team to elimination. Bartman, taking 100% of the blame, quickly became the most hated man in Chicago.

14. Patrick Kane brings Blackhawks first Stanley Cup in 49 years

June 9, 2010

The Blackhawks won their first Stanley Cup in 49 years in a very confusing fashion. The club was caught in a tense overtime against the Philadelphia Flyers, and Patrick Kane fired a shot from a seemingly impossible angle. The puck got caught in the top corner of the net, leaving onlookers confused as to what had happened. Kane immediately threw his gloves off and began dancing across the ice. After a brief video review, officials verified that the Blackhawks were Stanley Cup champions, sending relief to many lifelong fans waiting for this moment to happen again, and it was just a storybook beginning for the Blackhawks.

13. Sammy Sosa hits home runs 61 and 62, passes Maris

September 13, 1998

The 1990's and early 2000's was considered one of the best eras of baseball. in 1998, Sammy Sosa and rival from the St. Louis Cardinals Mark McGwire found themselves in a home run race for the ages. In what seemed like a back and forth display of power night in and night out, both players were looking to break the single-season home run record set by Roger Maris in 1961 with 61 home runs in a single season. With McGwire passing Maris' record just 5 days earlier, it was then Sosa's turn to shine. In the same game Sosa belted home runs number 61 AND 62, tying and passing Maris as well. This was the capstone of an amazing season for baseball, only to be unearthed and shunned several years later due to many players caught using steroids and performance enhancing substances. Sosa finished 1998 with 66 home runs, where McGwire set the record at 70, only to fall a few years later to another controversial PED user, Barry Bonds, whose record still stands at 73 home runs in a single season.

12. Walter Payton breaks the all-time rushing record

October 7, 1984

Considered by many as the greatest running back, and by some the greatest football player of all time, Chicago Bears running back Walter Payton became the NFL’s all-time rushing leader in 1984, breaking the record Cleveland’s Jim Brown set in 1965. In front of 53,752 people at Soldier Field, Payton carried the ball for 154 yards and finished the game well beyond Brown's old record. During the second quarter of the game, Payton made nine runs and gained 30 yards, including a one-yard touchdown with three seconds left in the half. To beat Brown’s record, he needed to carry the ball just three more yards. Almost as soon as the second half began, Bears quarterback Jim McMahon pitched the ball to Payton in a reliable play that the team called the “Toss 28 Weak.” Fullback Matt Suhey and left guard Mark Bortz protected Payton as he completed a record-setting six-yard run. He didn’t take much time to celebrate, though. All week, Bears officials had been urging their star to let them stop the game for a ceremony when he finally broke the rushing record, but Payton maintained that the team’s momentum was more important than his achievement. So, the festivities were brief. Payton accepted his teammates’ congratulatory hugs and handshakes, turned the record-setting football over to a representative from the Pro Football Hall of Fame, and went back to the game. (He did take a call from President Ronald Reagan afterwards.) His dedication paid off: The Bears won the game 20-7 and Payton set one more record, for carrying the ball more than 100 yards for the 59th time in his career.

11. "The Shot"

May 7, 1989

In 1989, Michael Jordan had yet to win an NBA title and was still trying to prove himself as one of the league's best players. In the Bulls' 1989 first round playoff series against the Cleveland Cavaliers, he quieted some of his critics. The Bulls were down by one point with three seconds remaining in the game. Jordan took the inbound pass and hit a buzzer-beating shot to bring the team into the second round to the playoffs. The Bulls did not win a title that season, but "The Shot" became remembered as one of Jordan's most clutch moments in his career. It was replayed on television hundreds of times, to the agony of Cleveland fans and the ecstasy of Bulls supporters. It was only one basket, yet it played an integral role in the fortunes of not one but two NBA franchises.

10. Kerry Wood strikes out 20

May 6, 1998

Few pitchers had the phenom hype like Kerry Wood had in 1998. In only his fifth major league start, he did something that still has yet to be beaten until this day. Facing the Houston Astros, who at the time were one of the best hitting teams in the league, Wood had a dominating day. The Astros managed one infield single and Wood hit a batter, so it wasn't a perfect game or even a no-hitter, if it was, it might have been the greatest game ever pitched. This sparked the career of Kerry Wood and a short time where the Cubs had real hope at a title run. You can see and hear the electricity in the air that day in the video above. This was an exciting time in Cubs history, and one that will always be remembered. Wood never did quite live up to the full hype however, as arm injuries really affected his career from then onward.

9. Bears dominate the Redskins 73-0

December 8, 1940

In the ultimate case of a revenge game, the Bears had their second meeting with the Washington Redskins in 1940. The first meeting was just a few weeks earlier, as the Bears lost 7-3. The Redskins coach, George Preston Marshall, told reporters that Halas and his team were “quitters” and “cry babies.” Halas used Marshall’s words to galvanize his players, and the Bears scored 78 points in their next two games to set up a showdown with the Redskins in the league’s championship game on December 8, also in Washington. The bears came out of the gate firing, and never looked back. At the end of the bloodbath, the Bears were on top 73-0, and were world champions. This started a 1940's Bears dynasty where they won championships four times in seven years. When Washington Redskins quarterback Sammy Baugh was asked if a dropped touchdown pass earlier in the game would have affected the outcome or change momentum, he stated: "Sure. The final score would have been 73–7."

8. Devin Hester- super run caps a ridiculous season

February 4, 2007

Devin Hester is now regarded by most as the best return man of all time in the NFL. 2006 was his rookie breakout season, in which he scored five times on a punt or kickoff return, and then another from a missed field goal. He was a large reason the 2006 Bears were a team to be feared. Even when he didn't score, he always put the Bears in great field position to score from just a short amount of yardage away. He became so feared, that teams would specifically plan each week not to kick the ball to him, instead opting for short kicks or kicking out of bounds, which still led into the Bears' favor. When the Bears rode their defense and special teams to Super Bowl XLI, all eyes were on Hester as the opening kickoff took place. Amazingly, the Colts kicked the ball to Hester, and he made them pay, resulting in the first opening kickoff return in Super Bowl history. Bears players and fans alike were going nuts on the sideline and at home. The celebration was short-lived, however, as the Bears ended up losing to the Colts by a score of 29-17.

7. Michael Jordan's "Flu Game"

June 11, 1997

Game 5 of the 1997 NBA Finals was pivotal. The Bulls were tied 2-2 with the Utah Jazz in the series and needed a win to avoid facing elimination. Bulls superstar Michael Jordan was needed to help the team overcome the Jazz who posed a threat to the Bulls' finals streak. The big story coming into the matchup was a stomach virus or food poisoning that Michael Jordan had contracted the day before. At 2 a.m on Tuesday morning, Jordan called his personal trainer to his hotel room, where he was lying in the fetal position and sweating profusely. The Bulls' trainers told Jordan that there was no way he could play the next day. The Jazz had just won Games 3 and 4 to tie the series at 2 wins a piece, and a third consecutive win would give them the lead. The Bulls needed their leader for this critical game, and despite his sickness, Jordan got out of bed at 5:50 p.m on Wednesday, just in time for the 7 o'clock tip-off at the Delta Center. It's unclear how bad Jordan's sickness actually was, but it clearly showed as Jordan often looked like he was on the verge of collapse. Jordan didn't just play in the game, but he dominated in a way only Michael Jordan could, scoring 38 points and adding to his already prominent legacy. The Bulls won the game 90-88, and in the final seconds Jordan collapsed into the arms of another Bulls star, Scottie Pippen, leaving a lasting image of what would forever be called "The Flu Game". The Bulls won the following game, which clinched their second-consecutive championship and fifth overall.

6. Blackhawks win on home ice- become a dynasty

June 15, 2015

After a crushing defeat in the Conference Finals of 2014 by the LA Kings, the Blackhawks again made it in to the Stanley Cup finals in 2015. The Blackhawks, considered again one of the best teams in the NHL that year, finally had a chance to win their third Stanley cup in six years, a complete overhaul from their 49 year drought before. The titles in 2010 and 2013 were both clinched outside of Chicago, but the Hawks had the opportunity to snag the cup in Chicago for the first time since 1938. With the Hawks playing a tough Tampa Bay Lightning team, fans new victory was all but certain. The Lightning, as the club with the better regular-season record, held home-ice advantage in the series. With the Lightning taking a 2-1 series lead, it gave the Blackhawks something to be on their heels. The Hawks won game four at the United Center and game five in Tampa before returning home again to the United Center to finish the series out. In a tough game, the Blackhawks held the Lightning in check en route to a 2-0 victory. As the final buzzer sounded, pandemonium broke out inside of the United Center, giving fans something they had been waiting a generation for.

5. Michael Jordan's final shot wins sixth championship for Bulls

June 14, 1998

Michael Jordan's Bulls swan song was one to be remembered. The Bulls again found themselves in the NBA finals, looking for their sixth title and second three-peat. The Bulls again faced the formidable Utah Jazz. It was long rumored that Michael Jordan, Scottie Pippen and Phil Jackson would not be returning to the Bulls the following year. In what seemed like that last time the unit would be together, the Bulls went back and forth with the Jazz most of the series, as the Jazz had the same record coming in but a higher seed, giving them home field advantage. During game six, the Bulls' Scottie Pippen aggravated a back injury early in the game, which made the Bulls dependent on Jordan one last time. With time winding down, The Bulls and Jazz went back and forth in a close game. With victory almost in the hands of Utah, Jordan stole the ball away and dribbled down the court. With 10 seconds remaining, Jordan started to dribble right, then crossed over to his left. Jordan hit the 20-foot jumper to give the Bulls an 87–86 lead with 5.2 seconds left. In what would be his last shot in a Bulls uniform, Jordan was again immortalized as the clock wound down, giving the Bulls their sixth championship, leaving no doubt that they were one of the most dominating teams in NBA history. Jordan was named NBA finals MVP once again (he was also MVP the previous five times).

4. Mark Buehrle's Perfect Game

July 23, 2009

Mark Buehrle was considered a fan favorite pitcher for the White Sox for his entire tenure with the team. He was never considered an elite pitcher in the game, but he was considered one of the most consistent. Already having a no-hitter under his belt in 2007, and a World Series champion in 2005 with the White Sox, Buehrle was no stranger to the spotlight. Shortly into the July 23rd game against the Tampa Bay Rays, it became clear that Buehrle was going to have a special day. At the time, the Rays were tied for their second-highest on-base percentage (.343) of any team, so they were one of the least likely to allow a perfect game. Buehrle had plenty of run support that day, as Josh Fields contributed early with a grand slam. Buehrle's big day did not come without some drama though, as in the top of the ninth inning, the Rays' leadoff hitter Gabe Kapler hit a deep fly ball to left-center field, where DeWayne Wise made a spectacular catch, taking a home run away from Kapler and saving the perfect game for Buehrle. Wise was a defensive replacement prior to the ninth inning. The next batter struck out swinging. The final batter, Jason Bartlett, hit a ground ball to shortstop Alexei Ramirez, who threw it to first to finish off the history-making moment for Buerle. Hawk Harrelson, the long time White Sox play-by-play announcer, said that "under the circumstances one of the greatest catches I have ever seen in 50 years in this game". As Buehrle neared the perfect game, he told the audience: "Call your sons! Call your daughters! Call your friends! Call your neighbors! Mark Buehrle has a perfect game going into the ninth!". Buehrle became only the 18th player in history to record a perfect game at the time of his performance. There now have been 23 perfect games in history.

3. "17 Seconds"

June 24, 2013

The Blackhawks opened their 2012-2013 campaign with the best start to a season in NHL history. The team had a magical run through the playoffs, which included a historic comeback against the Detroit Red Wings. The Hawks were down by one goal against the Boston Bruins in the final two minutes of Game six in the Stanley Cup Finals. A loss would send the series to a win-or-go-home game seven. With 90 seconds left in the game, the team pulled goalie Corey Crawford for an extra skater, and Hawks captain Jonathan Toews assisted Bryan Bickell on a goal to tie the game. Just 17 seconds later, Dave Bolland scored on a rebound to clinch the Hawks' second Stanley Cup championship in four years. It is one of the most dramatic turnarounds in one game in Blackhawks history, and one that cemented the Blackhawks as a force to be reckoned with for years to come, especially in the playoffs. It was the first time a team had won the Stanley Cup in regulation time by overcoming a deficit in the final two minutes.
